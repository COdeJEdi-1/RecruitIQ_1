TONE GUIDE — Tech Department (Backend Engineering)

Voice: Direct, specific, and collegial. We write to engineers as peers, not candidates.
We describe the actual work, not an idealized fantasy. Avoid marketing language.

DO:
- Use second-person throughout: "You will own...", "You have built...", "You are comfortable..."
- Name specific technologies, frameworks, and tools — vague language signals a poor JD
- Describe what the team actually does and what success looks like in the role
- Acknowledge trade-offs and complexity honestly (e.g. "a legacy codebase you will help modernise")
- Keep bullet points to one sentence — tight and scannable
- Use active verbs: own, build, design, review, drive, investigate, instrument, ship

DO NOT:
- Use "rockstar", "ninja", "guru", "wizard", "superstar", "passionate", "driven", "self-starter"
- Write vague bullets like "work in a fast-paced environment" or "communicate well with stakeholders"
- List more than 6 must-have skills — if everything is required, nothing is
- Use exclamation marks or hype language ("Join our amazing team!")
- Inflate seniority: a Mid role is not a Senior role with fewer years required

Structure expectations:
- Role Summary: 3–4 lines. Describe the team, the product surface this engineer will own,
  and the scale or technical context that makes this role interesting.
- Key Responsibilities: 5–7 bullets. Real tasks, not aspirational fluff.
- Must-Have Skills: 5–6 bullets. Things the person cannot join without.
- Nice-to-Have Skills: 3–4 bullets. Real differentiators, not checkbox padding.
- What We Offer: optional. Only include if approved benefits text is available.

Tone examples:
  GOOD: "You will own the payment processing pipeline end-to-end, from API contract design through
        to fraud detection hooks, serving 2M transactions per day."
  BAD:  "You will work on exciting payment features in a collaborative environment."

  GOOD: "You have shipped production services in Python or Go and can explain the trade-offs
        between the two when asked."
  BAD:  "You are proficient in multiple programming languages."
